Awin Global · 2023–2025 · Case Study

From Last-Click to Last-Cause

How a causal measurement system revealed that Awin's brand awareness campaigns were adding volume but destroying lead quality — and how proving that finding twice, after leadership initially pushed back, restructured ~€500K in quarterly marketing budget toward channels with genuine incremental lift.

Geo-Lift HoldoutsIncrementality TestingCUPED Variance ReductionBudget Optimisation
01 — The Business Problem

The campaigns looked like they were working.

The question was whether they were causing anything.

Awin's internal marketing team was running campaigns across Google Search, referral programmes, and paid channels with a single goal: to drive new advertiser opportunities onto the platform and convert them into active clients. The baseline was strong — we were converting 64% of inbound opportunities across organic and direct channels. Campaign activity appeared to be adding to that pipeline, and on last-click attribution, the numbers looked healthy across the board.

But last-click has a structural blind spot. It tells you what touchpoint preceded a conversion, not what caused it. A prospect who had already decided to evaluate Awin, who had already been through an initial sales conversation, who was already deep in the funnel — they'd still get credited to whichever campaign they happened to click last. The question the data didn't answer: how many of these opportunities would have arrived and converted without any campaign contact at all?

"We were reporting campaign performance based on what came last, not what caused the decision. For a platform built on performance marketing principles, measuring our own acquisition on last-click was a contradiction we needed to resolve."

With approximately €500K in quarterly marketing budget being allocated across channels based on these signals, the cost of getting this wrong was real. Brand awareness campaigns were consuming a significant share of the budget — and no one had yet tested whether the conversion credit they were claiming had been earned.

02 — Why the Obvious Approach Wouldn't Work

Pausing a channel for a week tells you nothing in a B2B sales cycle.

The instinct in most marketing teams is to turn off a channel and watch what happens. In a B2B platform context where the advertiser sales cycle runs weeks to months, a short pause produces noise, not signal. Any effect would be buried in pipeline lag, seasonal variation, and the natural rhythm of a long conversion funnel. You need holdouts calibrated to the sales cycle length, not just the campaign flight window.

There was also an organisational constraint that mattered as much as the technical one. The brand awareness campaigns had strong internal advocates with years of reported performance data behind them. Any measurement approach that could be dismissed as too short, too narrow, or methodologically weak would simply get set aside. We needed a design that was both causally rigorous and institutionally defensible — because the results were going to face real scrutiny.

Awin operates across 10+ markets with different competitive dynamics, different sales cycle lengths, and different channel compositions. A single global measurement wouldn't generalise — we needed market-level incrementality estimates that could be aggregated reliably and interrogated market by market if challenged.

03 — What I Designed

A geo-lift framework built around the actual sales cycle, not the campaign window.

  • 1.
    Geo-lift holdouts across GSA marketsRandomised geographic holdout experiments across the GSA region, splitting comparable markets into treatment (campaigns running) and control (campaigns suppressed) groups. Markets were matched on pre-period opportunity conversion volume and seasonality profiles to ensure structural similarity before any experiment started. Each holdout ran for 4–6 weeks — long enough to capture a real conversion signal across the sales cycle, short enough to limit pipeline risk in suppressed markets.
  • 2.
    Isolating campaign type as the treatment variableRather than measuring "all campaigns" as a single block, I designed the holdouts to isolate brand awareness creative vs performance-focused creative as separate treatment conditions. This was the design decision that produced the most actionable finding: the incrementality gap wasn't uniform — it was concentrated almost entirely in brand awareness.
  • 3.
    CUPED variance reduction for sensitivityMarket-level experiments have inherently fewer statistical units than user-level tests. I applied CUPED using pre-period opportunity conversion metrics as covariates to reduce residual variance, meaningfully increasing the sensitivity of each experiment within the 4–6 week window — without extending holdouts to the point of creating unacceptable sales pipeline risk in suppressed markets.
  • 4.
    Production measurement platform in BigQuery, dbt, and LookerAutomated BigQuery pipelines pulling campaign, GTM tracking, and CRM conversion data; 12+ dbt models transforming raw events into standardised KPIs — opportunity CAC, conversion rate by source, payback period; and Looker dashboards giving marketing and product teams real-time self-serve access across markets. Reporting time dropped from a 2-day manual process to same-day. KPI definitions standardised across four growth teams who had previously been calculating them inconsistently.

The brand wasn't doing anything. It was adding volume and subtracting quality.

The first round of holdout experiments produced a finding more nuanced than a simple "brand doesn't work" conclusion — and more commercially significant because of it. When brand awareness campaigns were suppressed in control markets, inbound opportunity volume dropped approximately 12% in weeks 1–2. The channel was generating real volume.

But two other findings changed the interpretation entirely. First, the quality gap: brand-sourced leads were converting at approximately ~51% — a full 20 percentage points below the 64% baseline conversion rate from organic and direct opportunities. The campaigns were filling the top of the funnel with prospects who were structurally less likely to become clients.

The nuance that changed everything: Brand added ~12% volume but those leads converted at ~51% vs 64% for organic. Multiply volume × quality and net qualified opportunity output was close to zero. At ~€200K/quarter, that math mattered.

Second, and more conclusively, the recovery pattern. By weeks 4–6 of the holdout, the 12% volume drop in control markets had fully recovered. Organic and direct demand absorbed the gap naturally, without any campaign intervention. The volume brand awareness was generating wasn't incremental demand — it was latent demand arriving slightly earlier than it would have otherwise.

The net arithmetic: brand campaigns added ~12% volume at ~51% conversion vs a 64% organic baseline. Once quality-adjusted, the incremental output of qualified opportunities was close to zero — while consuming the largest single share of the quarterly budget.

05 — The Outcome

Leadership pushed back. We ran it again. The finding held.

The initial results met resistance. Brand awareness had strong internal advocates, and the finding — that the channel with the largest budget share had near-zero net qualified impact — was an uncomfortable one. Rather than relitigate the methodology, we reran the geo-lift holdouts with a second wave of experiments, extending the measurement window and adding market pairs to increase statistical confidence.

The finding replicated. The quality gap held at ~20 percentage points. The recovery curve appeared again. With two rounds of evidence pointing to the same conclusion, the conversation shifted from "is this right?" to "what do we do about it?" The result was a full reallocation of the ~€500K quarterly budget — total spend held flat, allocation rebuilt around channels with validated incremental lift and conversion quality at or above the 64% organic baseline.

Axel Springer National Media & Tech · 2022 · Case Study

22% Was a Lie. 15% Changed the Roadmap.

How auditing event-tracking pipelines across BILD and WELT revealed that the Registration→Trial conversion rate was being reported at 22% when the real number was 15% , a 47% inflation hiding the most significant leak in the subscription funnel.

Funnel AnalysisExperimentation DesignEvent Tracking AuditData Quality
01 , The Business Problem

The data looked right. That was the problem.

Axel Springer's digital properties , BILD and WELT , were making decisions every week based on funnel analytics and A/B test results. The tracking infrastructure and experimentation methodology underpinning those decisions had never been properly audited.

Duplicate event fires. Misfired tracking triggers. Tests were stopped the moment they crossed a significance threshold. Across five analytics teams, these weren't isolated incidents , they were systemic. And because nobody had surfaced them, the teams had no reason to question the numbers they were acting on.

"The most dangerous measurement failure isn't one that produces obviously wrong answers , it's one that produces plausible wrong answers. Numbers that look right, behave right, and get used confidently."

A product team with 22% showing in their dashboard has no reason to prioritise that step. A product team with the real number , 15% , has an immediate roadmap conversation.

02 , What Was Actually Broken

Two failure modes. One shared root cause.

The tracking failure. Misfired triggers were recording trial start events that hadn't actually been completed, inflating the apparent conversion rate at Registration→Trial. Duplicate event fires were double-counting certain user actions. All five analytics teams were working on data that had never been validated against source of truth.

The second failure was methodological. Teams were stopping A/B tests early as soon as results crossed a significance threshold rather than running to their pre-specified sample size. Stopping at first significance inflates false positive rates dramatically.

"The teams weren't being careless, they were being responsive. An underpowered positive result and a real positive result look identical in a dashboard. The only thing separating them is whether the test ran long enough."

03 , What I Built

Fix the foundation. Then trust everything built on top of it.

  • 1.
    Full event-tracking audit across BILD and WELTSystematically mapped every tracked event against its intended trigger, identified duplicate fires, located misfired triggers, and cross-referenced event counts against server-side conversion data across all five analytics teams.
  • 2.
    Automated data quality checks to prevent recurrenceImplemented automated quality checks validating event counts and alerting teams to tracking discrepancies before they contaminated live experiments. Shifted from reactive discovery to proactive detection at the point of data ingestion.
  • 3.
    Rebuilt experimentation methodology across five teamsMandatory sample size calculations before experiment launch, holdout controls, statistical power analysis setting minimum detectable effect sizes, and hard stopping rules. A feature test that had previously been called positive after 1 week and 800 users now required 5 weeks and 8,000 users.
  • 4.
    Multi-platform funnel dashboards on clean dataWith the tracking layer validated, built funnel dashboards surfacing true conversion rates at each step. The Registration→Trial drop-off became visible at its real scale for the first time.

22% reported. 15% real. A 47% inflation hiding the most important leak.

The subscription conversion journey ran six steps: Page view → Article view → Paywall hit → Registration → Trial start → Paid subscription. What the tracking data was hiding was the true scale of the Registration→Trial gap.

The paid subscription rate from trial starters held at approximately 10%. The product wasn't losing people inside the trial , it was losing them in the gap between completing registration and deciding to start one. That's a friction problem, not a value problem.

06 , What I'd Do Differently

Honest reflection.

The tracking audit found the problems reactively. If I were starting again, I'd run a full tracking audit as the first deliverable of any analytics engagement , before touching any experiment data, before building any dashboard.

The early-stopping problem was cultural as much as technical. Adding hard stopping rules was the right intervention, but in retrospect I'd have paired it with a concrete demonstration: here are the tests you called positive in the last six months, here is the probability that each one was noise. Understanding changes behaviour permanently.

Gorillas Operations · 2022–2023 · Case Study

€1 of discount, €1 of revenue , or not?

How causal uplift modelling and CLV-based segmentation revealed that ~28% of Gorillas' promotional spend in Germany and the Netherlands was being absorbed by customers who would have ordered anyway , and how two counterintuitive pricing findings reshaped how every promotional euro was targeted.

Uplift ModellingCLV SegmentationPricing StrategyCausal Inference
01 , The Business Problem

In quick-commerce, everyone receives a discount. That was the problem.

Gorillas was operating in one of the most aggressively promotional environments in European e-commerce , a market where customer acquisition cost was high, margins were razor-thin, and the competitive pressure to discount was constant. The promotional budget across German and Dutch markets was running at approximately €120K per quarter. Every customer , new, returning, loyal, or lapsed , was receiving similar promotional offers, regardless of whether the discount was actually changing their behaviour.

A high redemption rate from a loyal customer who was going to order anyway isn't a marketing success , it's a margin reduction on a sale you already owned. The question nobody was asking rigorously: for how many of our customers is this discount actually causing an order that wouldn't have happened otherwise?

"In a startup burning cash to win market share, every promotional euro needs to be creating incremental revenue, not subsidising behaviour that was already going to happen. The aggregate numbers couldn't tell us which was which."

02 , The Real Obstacle Wasn't the Model

Before any insight could exist, the data had to.

Gorillas had no single data warehouse. Customer records, order history, promotional redemption data, and pricing data lived in separate systems across markets , each city-level operation with its own schema, its own update cadence, its own level of completeness. The first month of this project wasn't modelling. It was data archaeology: tracing customer identifiers across systems, reconciling promotional exposure flags, and building the pipelines that made a unified analytical layer possible.

"Measurement infrastructure is not a precondition for analytical work , it is the analytical work, at least until it exists. In an environment without a warehouse, the data scientist who can build pipelines and run models is worth more than one who can only do the latter."

03 , What I Built

Segment first. Model response. Then price by market.

  • 1.
    Data unification pipeline across DE, NL, and UKBuilt consolidation pipelines standardising customer identifiers, order cadence metrics, and promotional exposure flags across city-level data sources. Germany and the Netherlands became the primary analytical markets.
  • 2.
    CLV-based customer segmentation using cohort analysisUsing cohort-based LTV models on the unified behavioural data, I identified three structurally distinct customer types defined by their behavioural relationship with pricing and promotions. Habitual orderers were worth approximately 3× a deal-hunter at 90 days.
  • 3.
    T-learner uplift models to separate response from coincidenceDeployed T-learner causal uplift models , training separate response models on treated and control populations, then computing individual treatment effects. The output was a propensity score per customer, estimating whether a promotion would cause an incremental order or simply be redeemed on a sale that was already happening.
  • 4.
    Pricing elasticity modelling across four levers and three marketsModelled price elasticity across delivery fee changes, minimum order value thresholds, product-level price changes, and promotional discount depth. Germany and the Netherlands showed near-identical elasticity patterns. The UK diverged significantly, requiring a separate model.

05 , What We Found

Bigger discounts didn't buy proportionally more orders. And €2 off delivery beats both.

Approximately 28% of customers in Germany and the Netherlands showed near-zero treatment effect , promotions had no meaningful causal impact on their ordering behaviour. At ~€120K quarterly spend, roughly €33K per quarter was being absorbed by customers whose behaviour wasn't changing.

A 20% discount drove almost the same incremental order volume as a 30% discount across promotion-responsive segments. The last 10 percentage points of discount depth were buying almost nothing , pure margin cost with no behavioural return.

"€2 off the delivery fee outperformed a 20% basket discount in Germany on incremental order conversion. Delivery fee reduction removes a binary barrier , 'do I pay anything to receive this order?' , while basket discounts adjust a continuous price variable. One changes the decision frame. The other changes a number within it."

06 , What Changed

Three segments. Three strategies. One budget that worked harder.

Habitual orderers were shifted out of standard promotional flows and replaced with retention-focused communications. Deal-hunters received promotions capped at 20% depth rather than 30%, recovering margin with no meaningful reduction in incremental order volume. Lapsed users received personalised win-back sequences with expectations reset around their likely post-reactivation value.

07 , What I'd Do Differently

Honest reflection.

The data fragmentation problem was the constraint that shaped everything else , and I accepted it as a given rather than treating it as the first thing to solve. If I were starting again, I would push for a centralised warehouse as a project prerequisite, not an ongoing workaround.

The lapsed user segment deserved its own dedicated controlled experiment. The surface signal (high reactivation rate) and the underlying reality (low post-reactivation CLV) took too long to reconcile. Building the 90-day CLV measurement into the reactivation campaign logic from day one would have changed how the segment was prioritised and funded.

The deal-hunter segment needed a CLV-adjusted promotional framework from day one. An incremental order from a deal-hunter was worth roughly one-third of a habitual orderer's equivalent order at 90 days. Building that adjustment into the uplift model targeting logic earlier would have prevented over-indexing on deal-hunter acquisition in city campaigns where the incremental volume looked strong but the long-run revenue contribution didn't justify the discount cost.
